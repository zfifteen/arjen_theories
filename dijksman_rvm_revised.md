# A Geometric Sketch Toward a Mechanical Origin of Inertia
### Arjen Dijksman (2003) — Revised and Corrected Edition

---

## Abstract

This paper presents a conceptual demonstration that inertia-like resistance to motion can emerge from purely mechanical collision dynamics between massless, rotating line-segment particles within the Rotating Vector Model (RVM). A single, geometrically constrained two-particle collision is analyzed to establish the existence of this mechanism. The results are explicitly scoped to the analyzed geometry; generalization to arbitrary collision configurations is proposed as a research hypothesis requiring further derivation. No claim is made that this demonstration constitutes a complete theory of mass or a derivation of mass-energy equivalence.

---

## 1. Introduction

Standard formulations of classical and quantum mechanics treat mass as a primitive input. A particle either has mass or it does not; the origin of that mass is not addressed within the mechanical framework itself. Two broad classes of sub-quantum theory attempt to recover determinism from an underlying structure:

- **Pilot-wave theories** (de Broglie, Bohm): particles are point-like or small spherical singularities guided by a wave. Mass is still presupposed as an intrinsic property of the guided particle.
- **Rotating or oscillating segment theories**: particles are modeled as extended geometric objects whose periodic behavior generates the wavefunction. This class includes superstring theory and the Rotating Vector Model (RVM) for fundamental particles.

The distinguishing feature of the RVM, if the mechanism demonstrated here survives generalization, is that inertia would not need to be presupposed. It would arise from the collision geometry of massless segments. This paper establishes the existence of that mechanism in one specific, idealized case and proposes the conditions under which generalization could be tested.

---

## 2. The Rotating Vector Model: Particle Definition

Within the RVM, a fundamental particle is modeled as a straight-line segment of constant length \(L\) that rotates about its center. The rotation is associated with the particle's probability wavefunction. The model is massless at the level of particle definition; no inertial property is assumed.

---

## 3. Collision Analysis: Scope and Constraints

To demonstrate the emergence of inertia-like behavior, a two-particle collision is analyzed under the following four simultaneous constraints:

1. The rotational speed of segment CD is zero (\(\omega_{CD} = 0\)).
2. Segment AB rotates in a plane exactly perpendicular to CD, with rotational speed \(\omega\).
3. The collision contact point is the geometric center of each segment.
4. The translational velocity \(V\) of CD relative to AB is perpendicular to AB at the moment of contact.

**Scope declaration:** These four constraints define a measure-zero subset of possible collision geometries. The analysis below is valid strictly within this constrained scenario. Whether the qualitative result persists under relaxation of any single constraint is an open question addressed in Section 6.

Since both particles are massless, they separate at the fixed velocity \(c\). The rotational velocity at the extremities of AB is:

\[
v_{\text{tip}} = \frac{\omega L}{2}
\]

---

## 4. Collision Regimes

Three dynamically distinct regimes arise depending on \(\omega\):

**Regime 1 — Instantaneous contact** (\(\omega < \frac{\pi c}{L}\)): AB and CD touch momentarily and separate without sustained interaction. The collision is effectively instantaneous.

**Regime 2 — Critical recollision** (\(\omega = \frac{\pi c}{L}\)): After initial separation, the B-extremity of AB reaches the trajectory of CD and a second, brief collision occurs. This deflects the final departure direction of CD but does not produce sustained contact.

**Regime 3 — Non-instantaneous contact** (\(\omega > \frac{\pi c}{L}\)): The contact point between AB and CD rotates faster than \(c\) about the geometric center of AB. AB actively pushes CD. The two segments glide against each other — the contact point progressing along AB toward one extremity — until that extremity is reached, at which point the particles separate at velocity \(c\).

## 5. Inertia-Like Behavior in Regime 3: A Constrained Demonstration

In Regime 3, the non-instantaneous contact duration is a function of \(\omega\): higher rotational speed produces longer gliding contact, which in turn produces greater deflection and resistance to the translational motion of CD.

**Within the constrained geometry of Section 3**, this sustained resistance to motion is structurally analogous to inertia. A qualitative proportionality is observed:

\[
\tau_{\text{contact}} \propto f(\omega, L, c)
\]

where \(\tau_{\text{contact}}\) is the gliding contact duration. The precise functional form of this relationship requires further derivation and is not established here.

**Physical interpretation (scoped):** If a rapidly rotating segment (candidate: electron, with high \(\omega\)) traverses a region populated by slowly rotating segments (candidate: photons or neutrinos, with low \(\omega\)), repeated non-instantaneous collisions would produce a cumulative resistance to translational motion. Within this picture, the rapidly rotating segment continuously deflects encountered slow segments, consistent with the generation of an electromagnetic field varying with acceleration or deceleration.

**What is not claimed:** This demonstration does not establish that \(\omega\) is proportional to mass in any quantitatively precise sense. It does not derive a scaling law. It does not constitute a derivation of \(E = mc^2\). These remain open research questions.

---

## 6. Generalization Hypothesis and Required Tests

The constrained demonstration of Section 5 supports the following hypothesis, which requires independent verification:

> **Hypothesis:** Non-instantaneous gliding contact, and the resistance to motion it produces, persists as a qualitative phenomenon across a broad range of collision geometries beyond the four-constraint special case.

To test this hypothesis, the following perturbations must be analyzed independently:

| Perturbation | Parameter varied | Test question |
|---|---|---|
| CD rotation | \(\omega_{CD}\) from 0 to \(\omega_{AB}\) | Does gliding contact persist at nonzero \(\omega_{CD}\)? |
| Collision plane | Tilt from perpendicular toward parallel | Does gliding contact persist at non-perpendicular angles? |
| Contact point | Shift from center toward extremity | Does gliding contact persist for off-center collisions? |
| Approach angle | \(V\) from perpendicular to oblique | Does gliding contact persist for oblique approach? |

If gliding contact duration remains a monotonically increasing function of \(\omega\) across all four perturbations, the special-case geometry is a valid representative instance. If it disappears or reverses under any single perturbation, the mechanism is geometrically fragile and the inertia analogy does not generalize.

A further required step is the ensemble model: a single two-particle collision cannot establish a bulk inertial property. A treatment of a rapidly rotating segment traversing a statistical distribution of slowly rotating segments — computing net cumulative resistance as a function of \(\omega\) — is necessary before any quantitative connection to mass can be proposed.

---

## 7. Toward a Mass-Energy Connection: Open Problems

Subject to successful generalization (Section 6), the following research problems arise:

1. **Scaling law derivation:** Determine the functional dependence of cumulative collision resistance on \(\omega\) from the ensemble model. Is it linear, quadratic, or otherwise?

2. **Dimensional analysis:** Establish whether the proportionality \(\text{inertia} \propto \omega\) is dimensionally consistent with the identification of \(\omega\) as internal energy.

3. **Mass-energy equivalence test:** Determine whether the derived scaling law is consistent with the form \(E = mc^2\). This cannot be assumed; it must be derived.

These are necessary conditions for the RVM to constitute a complete mechanical theory of inertia. They are not satisfied by the present analysis.

---

## 8. Conclusion

This paper demonstrates, within a single geometrically constrained two-particle collision, that massless rotating segments can produce non-instantaneous contact that is structurally analogous to inertial resistance. This existence result is the paper's primary contribution.

The following claims are explicitly not made: that this mechanism generalizes to arbitrary collision geometries, that \(\omega\) is quantitatively proportional to mass, or that mass-energy equivalence follows from these results. These remain open and well-defined research problems, whose solutions would determine whether the RVM provides a complete mechanical derivation of inertia.
